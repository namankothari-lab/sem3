Common Types of Film Cuts

A simple guide to help you understand the language of editing

Straight Cut

The most basic cut—a direct transition from one shot to the next. Used everywhere for clarity and pace.

Jump Cut

A sudden, noticeable transition. Often used to show time passing or to create a jarring, energetic effect.

Match Cut

A cut between two visually similar shots. Used for clever transitions and to draw connections between scenes.

Cutaway

A cut to something other than the main action, then back. Useful for context, reactions, or hiding edits.

Cross-Cut / Parallel Editing

Alternates between two or more scenes happening at the same time. Builds tension or shows connections.

L-Cut & J-Cut

L-Cut: Audio from scene A continues over the start of scene B.
J-Cut: Audio from scene B begins before you see it. Both make edits feel smooth and natural.

Smash Cut

A sudden, dramatic cut—often from something calm to intense (or vice versa). Used for surprise or humor.

Montage

A series of quick shots edited together, often set to music, to show the passage of time or development of a story.

Why do editors choose different cuts?

Each cut has its own rhythm and emotional effect. The best editors pick the right cut for the story moment!
